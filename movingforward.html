Moving Forward

Listen to the Audio Story

Becoming a Bilingual reporter requires more than fluency in two languages. It’s navigating uncharted territories. When Maria Palma, reporter with KUNR, began to do Bilingual work, it was a challenge. “I didn't have any manual or any person telling me this is how you do it. It's a lot of improvisation,” She said, “using a translator at first and then getting comfortable.”

This semester, Palma is mentoring Emilio Milo, a Spanish journalism double major student at the University of Nevada, Reno, in learning how to do Bilingual work. He was brought into the role by Vicki Adame, editor of KUNR, at the start of his internship. Milo said that it was difficult at first, having no prior experience in speaking Spanish professionally. “It's been a great experience helping translate, create scripts, and even record the episodes,” he said, “I'm still waiting on having the opportunity to edit, but I'm pretty much excited to say that I'm a part of that.”

Milo is also part of the current Noticiero Movil Cohort, creating stories for the outlet. This, he said, helped him combat the awkwardness of speaking Spanish. Milo's motivation to make this transition makes Palma hope that it won’t be the last time she gets help. “As we inspire more people like Emilio, now that he has his own experience with bilingual work and newscasting in Spanish, those people are going to inspire other people,” she said, “I'm personally very proud of doing this and inspiring new people and training students that want to learn more about bilingual journalism.”

This awkwardness to speak Spanish in a professional setting, Palma notes, creates a big barrier for young journalists to improve their Spanish. “We have so many people that are fluent in Spanish, and they don't use the skill because maybe they don't see it as important,” said Palma, ”And when you have somebody interested in journalism and Spanish doing journalism, I feel like people appreciate that. And it's only a matter of encouragement, of learning.”

Palma said that it’s important to fight the embarrassment one might feel making mistakes early on. “you get really shy and you have a bad experience maybe, and you just don't want to do things in Spanish just because you feel embarrassed” Palma said it’s important to remember the call to action, and purpose for doing Bilingual work, “this is who I am, this is the community I belong to, and this is the community I want to serve.”

Milo’s advice for those in a similar position is to expose yourself to Spanish more. “As someone who is still learning, just to throw yourself out there,” said Milo, “You might mess up grammatically. You might not understand some words or understand how to report these things, but trying to expose yourself to reporting in Spanish is the best way to start doing it.”

While this project focused on Spanish-speaking communities, this news desert exists for all ethnic communities. Maria hopes works like Al Aire and Noticiero Movil can inspire other ethnicities to do similar work “It will be cool if at some point if we have students that speak another language that is not Spanish and they're fluent and they want to do it, maybe we can have newscasts in other languages as well, and that would be cool, if we have other populations as well, why not?”

The future of local media depends on new journalists to commit and bring systemic change in the industry. Innovations in media like Al Aire and Noticiero Movil prove that there is a desire and a need to do Bilingual reporting. It means bridging the information gaps in communities, extinguishing the news desert, and creating a fertile ground for communities to thrive. Local media might have been the most impacted by the loss of classified ads, but it’s also the most resilient. New generations of reporters are inspired by these gaps and are committed to ensuring no residents are left in the dark.
