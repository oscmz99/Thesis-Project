Becoming a Bilingual reporter isn’t easy. But there is a huge payoff to being able to become that bridge that connects a community with what is going on in the world around them. In many cases, there isn’t a clear path on how to do Bilingual Reporting. Once Maria began the work on Al Aire, she had to figure out everything on her own.

“There's not like a manual. When I started doing bilingual work, I didn't have like any manual or any like person telling me this is how you do it. It's like a lot of improvisation, maybe using a lot of translator at 1st and then getting comfortable”

Maria Palma is still producing her own stories apart from her work with Al Aire. Which is why she was thankful for this semester getting help from fellow reporter, Emilio Milo, a Spanish-Journalism student at the University of Nevada, Reno.

When getting the internship with KUNR, he was asked if he would be interested in helping Maria.

“I started doing it after the first couple of weeks of my internship. Vicki Adame, our editor, mentioned me helping Maria Palma with Al Aire.”

Emilio says that it was difficult at first, not being used to speaking in Spanish, but that he’s learning a lot.

“ it's been a great experience helping translate, create the script, and even record the episodes. I'm still waiting on having the opportunity to edit, but I'm pretty much excited to say that I'm a part of that.”

Emilio is also a part of the current class, doing work with Noticiero Movil. He says that it was within the past year that he felt inspired to start creating bilingual work. While the university is only starting to build up its bilingual efforts, it’s beginning to inspire many young journalists.

Maria sees that Reno has come along way and hopes this won’t be the last instance of people helping out with Al Aire

” as we inspire more people like Emilio, now that he has his own experience with bilingual work and newscasting in Spanish, those people are going to inspire other people. And that's how it goes. And I'm personally very proud of doing this effort and inspiring new people and training students that want to learn more about bilingual journalism.”

Maria understands that it can be a challenge at first, when a student isn’t used to speaking in a different language, it might seem awkward at first, it’s the difference between being a heritage speaker and a native speaker. Emilio himself said that it was weird having to use Spanish in a professional setting; it’s different compared to speaking Spanish at home. This is the case for a lot of students; the education system doesn’t push to be bilingual, and so most students don’t practice Spanish professionally.

“ we have so many people that are fluent in Spanish and they don't use the skill because, maybe they don't see it as important. And when you have somebody who's interested in journalism and Spanish doing like journalism, I feel like people, appreciate that. And it's only a matter of like encouragement, of learning.”

It’s important for those seeking to do bilingual work not to get discouraged about their abilities. With practice, Maria says that you can eventually build yourself to find your voice in Spanish. Wanting to do the bilingual work, you understand that you have a mission to report on a community and give a voice to the community.

“you get really shy and you have a bad experience maybe, and you just don't want to use your Spanish or do things in Spanish just because you feel oppressed, you feel embarrassed, whatever, or you basically get stronger and be like, okay, this is who I am, this is the community I belong to, and this is the community I want to serve to.”

While Emilio has only just begun his journey in making bilingual work, and just like Maria, he’s had to figure out how to do it on his own, he has some advice for those looking to do bilingual work.

“as someone that is still learning, just to throw yourself out there still. You might do mess up grammatically. You might not understand some words or understand how to report these things in what might be like your mother language or the language that you kind of grew up doing, but not necessarily used in an educational or professional sense yet. But I think trying to expose yourself to reporting in Spanish is the best way to start doing it. It's a bit more of doing it is better than just fantasizing about it. And yeah, I think also consistently trying to catch up with news in Spanish as well.”

Ethnic media isn’t just limited to Spanish; it can be all types of languages. It’s important to talk about Latinos and the Spanish Media because it’s the second biggest group in the country, but there are other groups with their own communities and needs. Maria hopes that Spanish isn’t the only language that grows in media.

“it will be cool in like at some point, If we have students that probably speak another language that is not Spanish and they're fluent and they want to do it, maybe we can have newscasts in other languages as well, and that would be cool, that would be really inspiring to see. Not because it's not only like Spanish and English. I feel like if we have other populations as well, why not?”

The future depends on the young journalists to bring the much-needed change the industry needs. New concepts like Al Aire, still in its infancy, are already proving to inspire journalists like Emilio to realize their potential. Becoming a bilingual reporter isn't a specialty; it's a necessity. It's understanding that as a journalist, you fill the gaps that a community suffers from. At the end of the day, local news isn’t just a job; it's a service to the community you report on.
