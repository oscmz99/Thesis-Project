The Solution

Listen to the Audio Story

The role of a journalist is vital in converting the news desert into a fertile source of information for people. For smaller markets like Reno, where newsrooms are short-staffed, reporters become vital in closing the information gaps. When you work in a community, you become a part of that community.

Dunia Elvir an anchor with Telemundo in Los Angeles and president of the National Association of Hispanic Journalists, said that in smaller markets, reporters are integral. “You are more than just a messenger. You're the bridge. You are what connects the community with the information. You connect the community with education. You connect the community with advocacy.”

Newsrooms have to balance between recognizing the needs of a community and ensuring they don't take drastic risks that will alienate their current audience. Challenging the status quo threatens the stable revenue streams that drive outlets. For stations like KUNR, fully funded by public support, less revenue can lead to the loss of reporters. However it didn't stop the station from being innovative by identifying a problem and coming up with a solution.

In "Reporting on Latino/a/x communities: A Guide for Journalists" by Routledge, a chapter written by Vanessa Vancour, founder of Noticiero Movil, a bilingual digital media outlet at the University of Nevada, Reno, in 2015, said she partnered up with Michelle Billman, former editor of KUNR, who pitched the idea of having a paid internship between the two outlets. Vancour said, “Initially, the reporter focused on in-depth features centered on sports, business, health disparities, immigration, and art.” She said the position evolved to produce daily stories for the station.

The partnership gave the opportunity for the station to experiment and understand how to reach a more diverse audience. The partnership led KUNR to eventually launch a Spanish edition of its broadcast. When Maria Palma joined the station as a reporter, she recalls that it wasn’t until Vicki Adame arrived as editor that the station saw potential in creating the Spanish-language newcast. With Adame’s bilingual leadership and Palma’s background in bilingual reporting, having done reporting with Noticeiro Movil, Al Aire was established.

KUNR had some backlash at first. Palma said when Al Aire first premiered, “A lot of people were reaching out to us and saying, ‘We don't like hearing Spanish on the radio. We are going to stop donating because we don't like hearing Spanish on the radio’,” she said, “‘If we cannot understand the product, then why would we donate and support your mission?’” Despite the threats to its financial support, KUNR stayed committed to its goal, transforming from an English-only station to a vital source of information to the people of Northern Nevada. Palma said, “ I feel like two years after, we can really say, this is working.”

Palma is the sole producer of Al Aire, and to streamline the process, she repackages stories that have already been produced. She said, “I use the stories that my co-workers and my other reporters did throughout the week. And I translate those stories without losing any meaning."

To effectively combat a new desert, especially in the era of smartphones, media outlets must look beyond their traditional modes of broadcast and enter different spaces. Hilda Berganza, climate manager at the Hispanic Access Foundation, said that it's important to understand and be where Latinos are. “Where do Latinos go and where do they hang out? If there's a way to share information at the local Panaderia or Carniceria or the local public library, that's important for the media to have a presence there. It is very important to meet people where they are.”

The digital space echoes these physical hubs. Vancour recalled that in 2019, when bilingual intern Karina Gonzalez published her story with Noticiero Movil, Roughly 4,400 TPS Holders Await Their Fate, and was also published on KUNR, becoming the number one story on the site, with 97% of new visitors coming from Facebook, a platform that has become a digital hub for Latinos. Similarly, WhatsApp is the prime communication app for Latinos, with the Pew Research Center finding that in 2021 46% of Latinos were on WhatsApp. The New York Times reports that the reason for this high usage is primarily to connect with relatives back home.

KUNR understood the importance of having a presence on these platforms, so with the launch of Al Aire, they created a WhatsApp channel to deliver the newscast straight to people’s phones and establish an immediate feedback loop. “And so people engage with it,” said Palma, “they react with emojis, they send me a message back, and they say, thank you so much.”

The growth of Reno’s Latino market is mirrored at the UNR, Reno. Palma said, “It's great that the Reynolds School of Journalism at UNR is training or at least encouraging students to use their Spanish.” Attributing the growth to Noticeiro Movil's current editor, Claudia Cruz. Continuing the work of Vancour, Cruz has expanded the newsroom to record numbers of students and recently celebrated the outlet's 10th anniversary. “ I feel Claudia has been getting so many students interested in the work, and that's amazing,” said Palma, “Maybe I'm mistaken, but I believe Noticiero is like one of the biggest bilingual efforts in a college, student-led newsroom.”

Challenging the status quo also means breaking into roles held by English-only newsrooms. Leo Murrieta, director of Make the Road Nevada, said, “Latino journalists who work in English-language media play a really big role in making sure that our voices aren't exclusively banished to the Spanish language.” He insinuates that representation doesn’t stop at Spanish-only speakers; it extends to Latinos who didn't grow up speaking Spanish. “We have a lot of younger folks who rely on English language media because they don't necessarily have a strong grasp of the Spanish language, but they still deserve to have their voices heard.”

As Latinos break the barriers, representation in the media grows. The ability to speak two languages is integral in closing the news desert and building that bridge to the community. Reporting becomes more than a job; it's taking the call and becoming a service to the community.
