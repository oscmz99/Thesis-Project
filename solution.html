The Solution

The role of a journalist can seem daunting, especially when focusing on a specific community. However, there is more to being a journalist than making sure you have the headlines done. It’s filling in the gaps that are missed.

When you work in a community, you become a part of that community. Dunia Elvir, news anchor with Telemundo in Los Angeles and President of the National Association of Hispanic Journalists, said that in smaller markets, the role of a journalist is vital.

“you are more than just a messenger. You're the bridge. You are what connects the community with the information. You connect the community with education. You connect the community with advocacy,”

Understanding that there is a community that needs to be talked about is one thing; taking the risk to talk about them is an entirely different issue. There can be pushback to break the status quo, since it can risk alienating an audience.

KUNR always wanted to launch a Spanish edition of its broadcast. Maria Palama said that it wasn’t until Vicki Adame was brought in as editor that the station had both a reporter and an editor who spoke Spanish. The station understood the gaps in the community, and because Reno really didn’t have a huge Spanish media presence, they moved forward with their concept. They began with trial runs of the broadcast to figure out what worked and what didn’t work, and eventually Al Aire was officially launched. KUNR had some backlash at first.

“A lot of people were reaching us and saying, we don't like hearing Spanish on the radio. We're going to switch to another one. We are going to stop donating because we don't like hearing Spanish on the radio. If we cannot understand the product, then why would we donate and support your mission?”

the station always wanted a Spanish-language newscast, they kept producing the show, and eventually, Al Aire became a success.

“A lot of people were confused about it But now I feel like two years after, we can really say, this is working,”

For Maria, the process of producing the show is fairly simple

“what I do is I repackage. I use the stories that my co-workers, my other reporters did throughout the week. And I translate those stories without losing any meaning, without changing any fact.”

A simple but a successful formula that Telemundo Reno does in their daily broadcast. For one hour in the evening, they bring the important headlines of the daily and repackages them into Spanish. It’s all about educating the community, and sometimes just translating stories is enough.

To understand what stories are vital to the community, Hilda Berganza said that it's important to understand and be where Latinos are.

“Where do Latinos go and where do they hang out, you know? So if there's a way to share information at the local Panaderia or Carneceria or like, the local, public library, I think that's important for media to have a presence there I think that is very important also as well, to meet people where they are.”

The digital space isn’t that much different from the real world. WhatsApp is a really popular app among Latinos, with the Pew Research Center finding that in 2018, 49% of Latinos were on WhatsApp, primarily to connect with relatives outside of the USA. This makes it a no-brainer for companies looking to expand into the Latino markets to be on these apps. The book Reporting on Latino/a/x Communities: A Guide for Journalists has found that the Latino market is the biggest market in the USA, contributing 2.6 trillion dollars annually.

This makes it vital to have an active presence on an app like WhatsApp. With the launch of Al Aire, KUNR opened up a WhatsApp for people to join, and the same newscast that is produced is sent to the WhatsApp group. And this is in huge part how they know that the newscast is successful.

“So we do have our own WhatsApp channel and we send the news, we send the episode every Friday.”

Once a story gets sent in the group, Maria can see the feedback immediately

“And so people engage with it. I mean, they react with emojis, they send me a message back and they say, thank you so much.”

Despite initial pushback, Al Aire was able to fill the void that the community needed, and the listeners understood that. The community understands that it’s important to include the Latino voice, its population is growing, and so it’s a growing market in Reno. Likewise, the University of Nevada, Reno can see the growth and is starting to encourage its students to study Spanish along side it’s journalism program. Maria, also an alumni with the school, sees the change happening.

“It's great that the Reynolds School of Journalism at UNR is like training or at least encouraging students to use their Spanish.”

Notably, the anchor of the Spanish development at the school is through the bilingual digital media outlet, Noticiero Móvil, which celebrated 10 years in October 2025. Led by Claudia Cruz, it’s influencing a ton of students, often becoming the biggest Spanish media in Reno despite its part-time nature. Maria Palma attributes the growth of interest in bilingual reporting to Claudia and Noticiero Movil,

“I feel like Claudia has been getting so many students interested in the work, and that's amazing. Maybe I'm mistaken, but I believe Noticiero is like one of the biggest bilingual efforts in, you know, in a college, student-led newsroom.”

Even without speaking Spanish, Leo Murrieta feels that Latinos can make a difference just by being able to break into English-language media.

“Latino journalists who work in English language media play a really big role to make sure that our hint as voices aren't exclusively banished to Spanish language. We have a lot of younger folks who rely on English language media because they don't necessarily have a strong grasp of the Spanish language, but they still deserve to have their voices heard.”

At the end of the day, it’s not a job, it's a service to a community. It’s being able to see a problem in the community and report on it. As the population grows, so does the representation of Latinos in the Media. Those who can speak two languages and become Bilingual Reporters are integral; you become the bridge for the community.
