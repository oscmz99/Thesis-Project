Intro

The release of the iPhone in 2007 decimated the classified ads for the news media that subsidized local media. Research by the Stanford Graduate School of Business found that this loss of classified ads impacted coverage of local politics, because it cost papers more to produce political news compared to entertainment and sports. This revenue vacuum forced local media to consolidate or terminate, stripping communities of their voice.

The decline created what The Center for Innovation and Sustainability in Local Media defines as a news desert: a community, either rural or urban, with limited access to the sort of credible and comprehensive news and information that feeds democracy at the grassroots level. For ethnic communities whose primary language isn’t English, this crisis is amplified. In markets where newsrooms are already strained, the loss of a single outlet creates a substantial information gap.

Episode 1: The Problem

The 2020 U.S. census recorded nearly 65 million Latinos in the United States, constituting the country’s second-largest ethnic group. According to The Pew Research Center, nearly half of this demographic is U.S.-born. Furthermore, the census found Spanish to be the second most spoken language in the nation, with more than 40 million speakers.

With a demographic of this magnitude, Latinos require a robust media infrastructure to inform their community on issues impacting their daily lives. The State of Latino News Media, a project created in 2019 by the CUNY School of Journalism, analyzed and recorded the different news outlets serving Latinos. Of the 600 newsrooms identified, five states contained over half of these outlets: California, New York, Texas, Florida, and North Carolina. This leaves states like Nevada, which contains nearly a million Latinos, with a scarcity of Spanish-language media.

For the 100,000 Latinos living in Washoe County, there is an insufficient amount of coverage. Maria Palma is a reporter with KUNR Public Radio and producer of Al Aire, a Spanish-language newscast that airs every Friday. “It's probably 30% of Washoe County's population that speaks Spanish,” Palma said. “As long as we have those people living here, then services will exist. Services in their own language will exist. And we can't really ignore that we have people here speaking different languages.”

With Nevada’s media concentrated around Reno and Las Vegas, this leaves the rural communities in the state underrepresented. This news desert, Palma said, is created by the lack of reporters. “There are a lot of things going on in rural communities and other parts of the state, and we can't really get to them because we are only like 5, 4 reporters in the newsroom, so it's really hard,” she said.

The consequences of having a lack of personnel are notable in the areas of public health and climate change. When newsrooms are stretched thin, communities are more susceptible to environmental impacts due to the lack of hyper-local coverage. A study by the National Library of Medicine, “Toward Language Justice in Environmental Health Sciences in the United States: A Case for Spanish as a Language of Science”, determined that the information gap prevents Spanish-speakers from properly comprehending the effects of a changing environment.

Leo Murrieta is the director of Make the Road Nevada, a non-profit that assists Latinos in Nevada with a variety of issues, including environmental justice. He identified that Latinos concentrate together, a factor that makes them more vulnerable to environmental hazards. "East Las Vegas is where the overwhelming majority of the state's Latin population lives,” Murrieta said. “There's this huge rectangle here in Southern Nevada where I think something like 70% of the whole state's Latino population lives. And there is a drop. There's a literal valley within the valley here in Clark County, and that is where we live.”

This cluster of Latinos is significant because East Las Vegas is affected by an urban heat island. Research by the Desert Research Institute found that an urban heat island is characterized by concrete and a lack of green space, which traps heat from the atmosphere. A DRI study also found that in Reno, the neighborhood of Sun Valley, and the communities living alongside Wells Avenue are the warmest parts of the city.

These areas in Reno, similar to East Las Vegas, have a high population of Latinos suffering from the heat island. Apart from any discomfort or illness due to the extreme heat, it also creates a financial burden. The warmest areas of the city face higher cooling costs, intensified by the chokehold NV Energy has on its customers. Murrieta says that these people face the highest rates of energy in the state. “Real people who are having to decide between paying the light bill and paying their rent, and buying groceries,” Said Murreita.

Hilda Berganza, climate manager at the Hispanic Access Foundation, leads El Aire Que Respiramos (or The Air We Breathe in English), a project targeting cities around the country with concerning air quality and a high population of Latinos. Over the past three years, they’ve placed Environmental Protection Agency sensors throughout these cities to monitor the Particulate Matter. The EPA defines any microscopic particles under 2.5 micrometers that can enter the body and cause serious health problems.

In Las Vegas, particularly in Henderson, Berganza observed that the air quality is often affected by environmental events in neighboring states. “Henderson is really interesting in the sense that we have had huge spikes of PM2.5 and then really low levels. And it kind of is dependent on several things. So, any smoke that's coming from any of the neighboring cities or states.”

While a media outlet can provide coverage on these environmental events, such as the LA fires in 2025, the adverse effects of the soot that gets carried over might get overlooked. In a news desert, the health hazards that come from poor air quality impact the most vulnerable members of the communities, like children or the elderly.

“How do you feel not knowing that there's a wildfire near you? I don't know, right? Or like, hey, I heard that you have had asthma attacks since you were nine and they're getting worse. What is that like for you? And how is that impacting your medical bill?” Said Berganza, alluding to the fact that without proper information, people in these communities are not aware of these preventable health issues. If Latino families were more informed about how to combat horrible air quality, there would be less of a financial burden on these families, having to spend thousands of dollars on medical bills.

The CUNY J School project highlighted that even when news outlets exist, they don’t serve communities equally. This project didn’t take public comments; however, in gathering information, the students found that in a major market like the Bay Area, which has Latino news outlets, the focus is localized within the city limits. Suburban communities outside the city, such as those in Oakland, don’t feel equally represented.

A media outlet focuses on daily stories or the big headlines. Those stories are prioritized for immediate impact by an editor, who will choose to leave different stories for a different day but often leaves other stories behind, especially in a newsroom stretched so thin. In many cases, this creates a delayed reaction; in a state like Nevada, where communities are so vast, information can take weeks to arrive.

In overstretched newsrooms, editors have to prioritize stories with immediate impact, leaving other stories behind. In a state like Nevada with vast communities, the forgotten stories can create a delayed reaction to problems such as public health.

Without the attention of the media, the slack is often picked up by advocacy groups like Make the Road Nevada. For example, Murrieta launched a campaign in the City of Las Vegas to bring more attention to the lack of trees in the city. He said that, in addition to planting more trees, the types of trees being planted are important. “We primarily only plant male trees, which produce extremely high levels of pollen. And the air flows from the west to the east, which drives all of that pollen and that bad air quality over to the east side, where it sits on the ground and creates some of the highest rates of asthma in the entire state.” The campaign led to Murrieta working with the city arborist to ensure that, as they are redeveloping parts of the city, there needs to be more green space available for people to cool off and more female trees planted to help those more affected by pollen.

When newsrooms only have four to five reporters, this tree planting story doesn’t have the same weight; it will get left out unless there is an editor who prioritizes it. Once again, when stories like these are untold, they leave communities unaware of the hazards and suffering from preventable problems.

While these examples have primarily been focused on Nevada, the patterns can be seen all over the country. Just like the Oakland residents in the Bay Area market, the suburban communities on Long Island in New York, and Los Angeles, are overshadowed by the city center.

Berganza’s project echoes a similar concept.“Even if there's not an EPA sensor near you,” she said, “staying alert to what's happening in the environment is going to make a big difference for you, a family, and just your community in general,” she said. The issues are the same, the patterns repeat.

The underlying issue of a news desert is ensuring communities have the necessary information to thrive. “Not all of us have the same access to the internet or access to Spanish-speaking materials across the nation,” said Berganza, “So sharing that information with your loved ones if you see it, is super important because that's how we get the word out, and people are safe, especially as asthma rates and other rates can increase, as a whole, but in our community because of these things. So we just want to keep each other safe.” This is why Ethnic communities like Latinos are formed, to create a support system that helps one another.

Reporters can be the bridge that takes those stories and disperses them into a community. If the news desert environment widens, it could become difficult to close the gap, but having Spanish-language media in a community is fertile ground for a healthier population

Episode 2: The Solution

The role of a journalist is vital in converting the news desert into a fertile source of information for a community. For smaller markets like Reno, where newsrooms are short-staffed, reporters become vital in closing the information gaps. When you work in a community, you become a part of that community.

Dunia Elvir, both a news anchor with Telemundo in Los Angeles and President of the National Association of Hispanic Journalists, said that in smaller markets, reporters are integral. “You are more than just a messenger. You're the bridge. You are what connects the community with the information. You connect the community with education. You connect the community with advocacy.”

Newsrooms have to balance between recognizing the needs of a community and ensuring they don't take drastic risks that will alienate their current audience. Challenging the status quo threatens the stable revenue streams that drive outlets. For stations like KUNR, fully funded by public support, less revenue can lead to the loss of reporters.

In Reporting on Latino/a/x communities, Vanessa Vancour, founder of Noticiero Movil, a bilingual digital media outlet at the University of Nevada, Reno, in 2015, said she partnered up with KUNR in 2017 because of Michelle Billman, then editor of KUNR, who pitched the idea of having a paid internship between the two outlets. Vancour said, “Initially, the reporter focused on in-depth features centered on sports, business, health disparities, immigration, and art.” She said the position evolved overtime to produce daily stories for the station.

The partnership was a way for the station to experiment and understand how to reach a more diverse audience. The partnership led KUNR to eventually launch an entirely Spanish edition of its broadcast. Eventually, Maria Palma joined the station as a reporter and recalls that it wasn’t until Vicki Adame arrived as editor that the station saw potential in creating the Spanish-language newcast. With Adame’s bilingual leadership and Palma’s background in bilingual reporting, having done reporting with Noticeiro Movil, KUNR launched Al Aire.

KUNR had some backlash at first. Palma said when Al Aire first premiered, “A lot of people were reaching out to us and saying, ‘We don't like hearing Spanish on the radio. We are going to stop donating because we don't like hearing Spanish on the radio’,” she said, “‘If we cannot understand the product, then why would we donate and support your mission?’” Despite the threats to its financial support, KUNR stayed committed to its goal, transforming from an English-only station to a vital source of information to the people of Northern Nevada. Palma said, “ I feel like two years after, we can really say, this is working.”

Palma is the sole producer of Al Aire, and to streamline the process, she repackages stories that have already been produced. She said, “I use the stories that my co-workers and my other reporters did throughout the week. And I translate those stories without losing any meaning, without changing any fact.”

To effectively combat a new desert, especially in the era of smartphones, media outlets must look beyond their traditional modes of broadcast and enter different spaces. Hilda Berganza, climate manager at the Hispanic Access Foundation, said that it's important to understand and be where Latinos are. “Where do Latinos go and where do they hang out? If there's a way to share information at the local Panaderia or Carniceria or the local public library, I think that's important for the media to have a presence there. It is very important to meet people where they are.”

The digital space echoes the real world. Vancour recalled that in 2019, when bilingual intern Karina Gonzalez published her story, Roughly 4,400 TPS Holders Await Their Fate, the English version of the story held the number one position on the site, with 97% of new visitors coming from Facebook, a platform heavily used by Latinos. WhatsApp is another popular app among Latinos, with the Pew Research Center finding that in 2018, 49% of Latinos were on WhatsApp, primarily to connect with relatives outside of the USA.

KUNR understood the importance of having a presence on these platforms, so with the launch of Al Aire, they created a WhatsApp channel to deliver the newscast straight to people’s phones and establish an immediate feedback loop. “And so people engage with it,” said Palma, “they react with emojis, they send me a message back, and they say, thank you so much.”

The growth of Reno’s Latino market is mirrored at the UNR, Reno. Palma said, “It's great that the Reynolds School of Journalism at UNR is training or at least encouraging students to use their Spanish.” Attributing the growth due to Noticeiro Movil, now editor Claudia Cruz, celebrated 10 years with the outlet. “ I feel Claudia has been getting so many students interested in the work, and that's amazing,” said Palma, “Maybe I'm mistaken, but I believe Noticiero is like one of the biggest bilingual efforts in a college, student-led newsroom.”

Challenging the status quo also means breaking into roles held by English-only newsrooms. Leo Murrieta, director of Make the Road Nevada, said, “Latino journalists who work in English-language media play a really big role in making sure that our voices aren't exclusively banished to the Spanish language.” He insinuates that representation doesn’t stop at Spanish-only speakers; it extends to Latinos who didn't grow up speaking Spanish. “We have a lot of younger folks who rely on English language media because they don't necessarily have a strong grasp of the Spanish language, but they still deserve to have their voices heard.”

As Latinos break the barriers, representation in the media grows. The ability to speak two languages is integral in closing the news desert and building that bridge to the community. Reporting becomes more than a job; it's taking the call and becoming a service to the community.

Episode 3: Moving Forward

Becoming a Bilingual reporter requires more than fluency in two languages. It’s understanding that the job is navigating uncharted territories. When Maria Palma, reporter with KUNR, began to do Bilingual work, it was a challenge. “I didn't have any manual or any person telling me this is how you do it. It's like a lot of improvisation,” She said, “maybe using a lot of translators at 1st and then getting comfortable.”

This semester, Palma is mentoring Emilio Milo, a Spanish journalism double major student at the University of Nevada, Reno, in learning how to do Bilingual work. He was brought into the role by Vicki Adame, editor of KUNR, at the start of his internship. Milo said that it was difficult at first, having no prior experience in speaking Spanish professionally. “It's been a great experience helping translate, create the script, and even record the episodes,” he said, “I'm still waiting on having the opportunity to edit, but I'm pretty much excited to say that I'm a part of that.”

Milo is also part of the current Noticiero Movil Cohort, creating stories for the outlet. This, he said, helped him combat the awkwardness of speaking Spanish. Milo's motivation to make this transition makes Palma hope that it won’t be the last time she gets help. “As we inspire more people like Emilio, now that he has his own experience with bilingual work and newscasting in Spanish, those people are going to inspire other people,” she said, “I'm personally very proud of doing this effort and inspiring new people and training students that want to learn more about bilingual journalism.”

This awkwardness to speak Spanish in a professional setting, Palma notes, creates a big barrier for young journalists to improve their Spanish. “We have so many people that are fluent in Spanish, and they don't use the skill because maybe they don't see it as important,” said Palma, ”And when you have somebody interested in journalism and Spanish doing journalism, I feel like people appreciate that. And it's only a matter of encouragement, of learning.”

Palma said that it’s important to fight the embarrassment or even the oppression one might feel making mistakes early on. “you get really shy and you have a bad experience maybe, and you just don't want to do things in Spanish just because you feel oppressed, you feel embarrassed” Palma said it’s important to remember the call to action, and purpose for doing Bilingual work, “this is who I am, this is the community I belong to, and this is the community I want to serve to.”

Milo’s advice for those in a similar position is to expose yourself to Spanish more. “As someone who is still learning, just to throw yourself out there still,” said Milo, “You might mess up grammatically. You might not understand some words or understand how to report these things, but I think trying to expose yourself to reporting in Spanish is the best way to start doing it. It's a bit more of doing it is better than just fantasizing about it.”

While this project focused on Spanish-speaking communities, this news desert exists for all ethnic communities. Maria hopes works like Al Aire and Noticiero Movil can inspire other ethnicities to do similar work “It will be cool in like at some point if we have students that probably speak another language that is not Spanish and they're fluent and they want to do it, maybe we can have newscasts in other languages as well, and that would be cool, I feel like if we have other populations as well, why not?”

Closing

The future of local media depends on new journalists to commit and bring systemic change in the industry. Innovations in media like Al Aire and Noticiero Movil prove that there is a desire and a need to do Bilingual reporting. It means bridging the information gaps in communities, extinguishing the news desert, and creating a fertile ground for communities to thrive. Local media might have been the most impacted by the loss of classified ads, but it’s also the most resilient. New generations of reporters are inspired by these gaps and are committed to ensuring no residents are left in the dark.

Methodology

The goal of this project is to research and highlight the Nevada news desert, following a national trend of local media disappearing across the U.S., leaving communities without a voice. For this project, a news desert is best defined as a geographic zone with little to no access to media. The focus of this research is on the Spanish-speaking population in Nevada and how their vulnerabilities can be linked back to the lack of media providing information in their language.

Sources for this project were selected for either their professional experience working as a journalist or expertise in the topic of Latino climate vulnerabilities. Hilda Berganza is a climate manager with the Hispanic Access Foundation, and I had previously talked to her about Latino conservation and the importance of Latinos to care for and learn about the environment around them. In that interview, she told me about the Project she leads, El Aire Que Respiramos, which examined different cities in the U.S with high populations of Latinos and identified the hazards in the air. Leo Murrieta is a Director with Make the Road Nevada, a non-profit that helps Latinos navigate issues on topics such as Climate Justice. Make the Road Nevada specializes in advocacy and bringing about policy change in Nevada. Dunia Elvir is an anchor with Telemundo and the President of the National Association of Hispanic Journalists, which is a non-profit that is committed to the development, recognition, and representation of Hispanic journalists in the industry. Maria Palma is a reporter with KUNR who is the sole producer of Al Aire, a Spanish-language newscast that runs down the major stories of the station once a week. Emilio Milo is an intern at KUNR who is under the guidance of Maria and is learning how to do Bilingual reporting.
