Methodology

The goal of this project is to research and highlight the Spanish language news desert in Nevada, following a national trend of local media disappearing across the U.S., and how those impacts are affecting the local Latino community regarding climate issues. For this project, a news desert is best defined as a geographic zone with limited access to media. I used the case study of KUNR and how they developed their Spanish language newscast, Al Aire, and how that is being used to address the lack of Spanish language news in Reno. This is relevant because KUNR saw this lack of information being provided in Spanish and looked at a way to address the issue by partnering with Noticiero Móvil and learned how to reach the Latino audience in Reno, which led to the creation of Al Aire and its WhatsApp channel in Spanish.

The target audience for this project is the Latinos studying journalism, who may or may not understand the Spanish language. The hope is that these students are inspired to pursue and focus on the topics that are important to the Latino community and or learn how to do bilingual reporting. The decision to make this a multimedia project was based on the skills I learned throughout my studies. This is a capstone project, and I want to highlight my expertise in creating an engaging narrative story and converting it into an audio story. As well as showcasing my knowledge in web development and integrating UX and responsive web design.

For sources, I knew before the project that I wanted to talk to Hilda Berganza, climate manager at the Hispanic Access Foundation. She leads a project called El Aire Que Respiramos, selecting 12 different cities across the country that have a high population of Latinos and a concerning air quality. Her expertise on air quality and understanding of the Latino community made her vital in both talking about the impacts of poor air pollution in a city, how the lack of information affects an individual and the role the media should be playing in combating the issue. Leo Murrieta, director of Make the Road Nevada, was selected due to the non-profit’s goal in advocating for the rights of Latinos in Nevada. Among the many issues they advocate for, Make the Road Nevada specializes in climate justice, and so Murrieta has experience in campaigning and making changes to policy to help combat the climate issues that Nevadans face, as well as understanding how important it is for Latinos to be in journalism. Dunia Elvir, president of the National Association of Hispanic Journalists, came to the University of Nevada, Reno, to talk about how she navigated the industry. She comes with 36 years of experience, and also being president of NAHJ, she understands the role a Latino journalist should have in navigating the news desert. Her talks included what role a reporter should play in a small market. Maria Palma is another professional, and her expertise comes from being the sole producer of Al Aire for 2 years and knowing how the newscast was formed and the issues that came with its inception. Lastly, Emilo Milo was beginning his position of helping Palma with producing Al Aire and still an early career journalist, his voice was critical in knowing what it’s like for someone who isn’t used to speaking Spanish getting to use the language professionally.

The conclusion gathered from this project shows that there is a need for Latinos to access information vital to them. The news desert is impacting Latinos’ understanding of environmental stressors and research shows that this lack of understanding impacts their ability to properly assess their situation. KUNR has immediate feedback with Al Aire with the creation of the WhatsApp channel. Their success can be attributed to the comments they receive from listeners. As well as the continued partnership between KUNR and Noticiero Móvil, showing how KUNR understands the importance of the mission Noticiero Móvil has. Finally, the biggest conclusion is that the news desert isn't an inevitable fate; it's a temporary information gap that newsrooms can close when they become innovative, non-profits like Make the Road Nevada and the Hispanic Access Foundation are doing their part in educating a community, but the media can play a bigger role in providing the information that isn't being shared to Latinos.
